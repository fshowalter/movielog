Australian film historian Lee Gambin delivers a dense, well-researched commentary track that covers the production history, the source material's structural problems, and the studio politics behind Paramount's misfire. There is very little dead air. There is also very little spontaneity.

For most of the runtime, Gambin reads. From his own script, from contemporary reviews, from a Gary Cooper biography, from his own published work. You can hear the pages turning, figuratively if not literally. It's only in the final minutes, when the prepared material runs out, that a few "uhs" and "ums" creep in, and Gambin starts actually talking. He's more engaging in those last unscripted moments than in the preceding hour.

That said, he's an enthusiastic fan, which helps. His account of the challenges inherent in Carroll's work is genuinely interesting. The novels lack a conventional three-act structure, a problem serious enough that Walt Disney abandoned a proposed live-action/animated hybrid with Mary Pickford as Alice just before Paramount snatched up the rights. 

Less convincing are some of his critical judgments. He praises the film for presenting themes of Victorian social critique and childlike wonder that are simply baked into Carroll's text. The movie didn't create those themes. It inherited them, then did nothing interesting with them. He also rates the special effects above <span data-imdb-id="tt0024216">_King Kong_</span> and <span data-imdb-id="tt0024184">_The Invisible Man_</span>.

Gambin frequently cites the 1951 Disney adaptation, praising it at length. He's not wrong, but there's something funny about a commentary track that keeps directing you toward a better movie.

One note of disagreement with Scott Eyman: Gambin places Grant inside the Mock Turtle costume. Eyman says he only dubbed the voice. Somebody is wrong, and Gambin doesn't cite his source.

But Gambin earns his keep with one genuinely surprising insight. He posits Batman as an Alice analogy. Not just because Batman's rogues gallery includes a character literally called the Mad Hatter, but because the gallery itself functions like Wonderland: each villain a distorted reflection of the hero, each encounter a trip deeper into an unstable world. It's a sharp observation that extends well beyond the obvious, into how Carroll's paradigm seeped into popular culture in ways we stopped noticing. 

It's the kind of thought that makes you glad you listened.
