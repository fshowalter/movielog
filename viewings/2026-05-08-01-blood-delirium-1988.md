Eugenio Ercolani and Troy Howarth don't pretend _Blood Delirium_ is a lost masterpiece. They don't dismiss it, either. What they do, and do well, is place it within the crumbling landscape of late-1980s Italian genre cinema. The old guard was dying or dead. Fulci was fading. Mario Bava was gone. Argento was about to head to America while the new generation--Lamberto Bava, Michele Soavi--couldn't find an audience. Writer-director Sergio Bergonzelli was a veteran scrounging for work, turning to gothic exploitation because nothing else was hiring.

The commentary is rarely scene-specific. It functions more as a cultural history lesson with a movie running underneath it. One of its sharpest observations concerns the film's willingness to look Italian. This was a period when Italian productions did everything possible to pass for American, setting their stories stateside or hiding behind anglicized pseudonyms. Fulci's final film credited him as H. Simon Kittay. That _Blood Delirium_ embraces its own geography counts, in this context, as a minor act of defiance.

The recording, unfortunately, was done over Zoom, and it shows. Ercolani leads, but the telltale pitch warble creeps into his voice at least once. Worse, the format kills the conversational rhythm. Nobody interrupts. Nobody riffs. The participants pass the baton politely and wait their turn, which makes the whole thing feel like a lecture rather than a discussion. 

Still, I found it more entertaining than the movie.
